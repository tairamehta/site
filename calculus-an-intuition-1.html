The three main pillars of Calculus include:
Derivatives
Integrals
The fact that (1) and (2) are opposites
The best way of thinking about it comes from deriving the area of a circle.
Here, as DR gets smaller, and instantaneous, the value of the area gets more accurate. The area of a circle can therefore be defined as

Hard problems in mathematics can often be solved by breaking them down into smaller values. This can look like the distance of a car through change in velocity over time.
Derivatives, from this perspective, are the instantaneous change in a function. Integrals are the attempt to measure the area under any curve as a means to solve mathematics. In essence, calculus is the mechanism for which we can break down difficult concepts into the sum of many small things and use hard math to get an answer for it.
2. The Paradox of the Derivative
Derivatives measure instantaneous rates of change, yet this is an oxymoron. This, however, was intentional.
Take an example: The displacement/distance of a car over time

3. Derivative Formulas Through Geometry
Derivatives are fundamentally about looking at tiny changes in one quantity and relating it with tiny changes in another quantity. We don't often look at applications such as v(t) graphs at first due to the fact that many real world models are based off of trig, and exponential functions. Knowing them first therefore helps us later on.
